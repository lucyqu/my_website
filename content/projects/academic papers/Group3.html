Econometrics Case Study:

What Fires Your Anger

 –  A Multivariate Regression on the Factors of Medical Disputes in China

Na Q., Mengting C., Sixuan L.

Abstract

    This paper seeks to explain the underlying reasons behind surging medical disputes in recent China. We proposed the leading factors from both supply and demand perspectives based on classical literature and the regional descriptive facts. The significance of each factor is tested by linear or ordinal logit regression. According to our findings, the insufficient compensation scheme despite an extreme working pressure tends to be the key concern for doctors. While for patients, the difficult access to quality hospitals, misunderstanding about treatment results, and the ascending medical costs are giving rise to most of their anger.

1. Introduction

    As is universally acknowledged, China has witnessed a surging trend in medical disputes since the beginning of 21st century (Zhang et al. 2017). According to the WHO’s definition in 2002, typical medical disputes consist of four types - medical malpractice, medical litigation, medical violence, and medical assessment respectively. In China, the frequency of medical litigation hiked exceptionally rapidly recent years, and the outcome of each single dispute case is turning increasingly hazardous - the medical cases accepted by intermediate people’s court doubled in the past 15 years, and the portion of those extreme lawsuits where doctors were attacked to death went through significant growth (Fig1). The damaged doctor-patient relationship not only cast a shadow on the public healthcare system stability, but also drove down the overall social trust level. This pessimistic fact could be directly reflected from the diminishing volume of medical student enrollment and deteriorating scores in CGSS doctor trust  level assessment.

2. Hypothesis

2.1 Key assumptions

    Most cause analysis in this field suggest a demand and supply approach and we follow this logic. On the one hand, for the doctors as service suppliers, we assume the long working hours despite poor compensation are likely to depress them. On  the other hand, for the patients as treatment demanders, we suspect the hard access to Grade A hospitals, the rising medical costs with deficient insurance coverage, along with the excessive expectation about treatment outcome are shaping the bulk of their dissatisfaction.

2.2. Ratoinale for assumptions

    For the working hours and compensation factor, the rationale could be justified by a comparison between HK and mainland doctors. Despite a similar working schedule, HK doctors receive 7 times as high as the mainland doctors on average. Correspondingly, the mispute level in HK is also lower.

    For the accessibility factor mechanism, the regional distribution of Grade A Top 3 hospitals nationwide showed us a primary resource mismatch. As the provincial population density is inconsistent with the local hospital volume in some regions, many patients are forced to travel long journeys for effective treatments, which makes them much more easier to be emotional and irritated because of the large sunk cost. As a result, the medical conflict level in these regions are possibly intensified.

    For the medical cost story, we notice that the growth rate of domestic annual medical expenditure has significantly outpaced that of the disposable income since year of 2011(Fig. 2). At the same time, the social medical insurance on critical illness coverage maintained on low level. In this way, we suspect Chinese patients are becoming increasingly unable to afford the due care and consciously result in more conflicts.

    Lastly for the treatment expectation factor, literature suggests that the bulk of fatal attacker in violent cases are featured with low education level and ignorance about normal healthcare procedures. Since the resuscitation success rate in recent year has maintained a high level above 85%, it’s reasonable for us to assume that it is the unclear understanding about treatment rather than the poor treatment quality itself that is leading to the medical avalanche.

3. Method and Data

3.1 Method

    To test the hypotheses, we adopt the method of cross-regional comparison - first descriptive analysis, then regression models.

    For the descriptive part, we use chart graphs and maps to first illustrate how different regions vary themselves on the pharmaceutical-related facts (e.g. scores on doctor trust level, volume of Grade A Top 3 hospitals). We further identify any similar spatial pattern between these maps with the intention to identify possible correlations. For example, a similar distribution between low doctor trust level and low quality hospital volume could, to some extent, validate hospital volume to be a potential contributor to the frequent medical disputes.

    For the regression analysis, we use the same set of regional independent variables (e.g. hospital volume, medical costs) to accurately test their effects on the regional concern and individual perception about medical disputes (e.g. scores on doctor trust level).

 

3.2 Independent variables and data source

    Regarding the variables, we choose our key independent variables to be doctors’ burdens, medical costs and concentration of authoritative hospitals based on the previous assumptions. The data used for these independent variables are statistics in 32 provincial administrative regions extracted from China Health and Family Planning Statistics Yearbook in year 2011 and 2016. For doctors’ burdens and medical costs, we directly use the data of daily visits per doctor and average cost per outpatient/inpatient visit. For concentration of high-level hospitals, the number of Grade III Level A hospitals is further normalized with population size in the province in per 10,000 population scale.

 

3.3 Dependent variables and data source

    As discussed in previous sections, the focus of this research is the severity of medical disputes between doctor and patient groups as well as the social influence of this emerging phenomenon. Therefore, instead of the actual number of medical disputes happened, we choose the attitudes and concerns to related aspects of medical disputes as the dependent variables of our interest. Two proxies, respectively on the regional and individual level, are chosen for the analysis.

    For the regional level analysis, Baidu Index searching for keywords “medical disputes” (“医闹”) in the past half year is selected to reveal the local attention through media. Baidu Index allows users to look up the search volume and trend for certain hot keywords in particular regions within a period of time. After normalized to searches per million internet users, it becomes a proper proxy of public concern about medical disputes in each province.

    For the individual level analysis, we choose the 5618 responses to value questions in CGSS 2011 with subject of attitudes toward physicians as the measurement of perception about doctor-patient relationship. The four main questions extracted are “If something goes wrong in the treatment, doctors will tell patients the truth”, “Doctors will discuss all the possible treatment plans with patients”, “Doctors care more about their income rather than their patients”, and “In general, doctors can be trusted” (Fig 5). The respondents are presented with five-level Likert scale questions to choose their agreement level to statements listed above, with Number 1 representing strongly agree and 5 strongly disagree. The scale rated by each respondents, thus indicate their trust (or mistrust in the other way) to doctors as an occupation group.

    Another factor influencing doctor-patient relationship we proposed above is the patients’ perception of treatment outcome. From CGSS2011 the answers to a seven-level rating scale question “Are you satisfied with your last visit to the doctor’s?” are extracted and used as independent variables in the extended part of our research. The details and the rationale of choosing this variable will be further explained in the regression result part.

3.4. Control Variables and data source

    Moreover, in regression analysis, different control variables are used regarding to different dependent variables and regression models. The regression using CGSS responses is based on individual level, therefore we control personal characteristics such as disposable income, years of schooling, age, gender and disease history, whose data are available in the same survey. Since medical insurance plays an important role in most medical services, we also select information about medical insurance coverage of individual in the CGSS survey as controls. The results and effects of medical insurance are further explored in the later sections. For the regression of Baidu index, we control local disposable income level and regional average years of schooling for similar principles in the individual regression.

4. Findings

4.1 Descriptive analysis

Figure 6. Mapping of CGSS 2011 Attitudes Questions

    Figure 3 reports the density of internet searches for medical disputes (“医闹”) by Baidu Index per 10,000 population, while Figure 4 shows the province ranking of concentration of Grade 3 Level A hospitals. Comparing these 2 graphs, we observe that the regions with most intense concern and searches of medical disputes (deep green on the map), such as Beijing, Tianjin, Shanghai etc., coincide with those of high concentration of Grade 3 Level A hospitals (Beijing: Rank 1, Tianjin: Rank 2, Shanghai: Rank 7 in 32). Compared with their neighbor provinces (Hebei: Rank 31, Jiangsu: Rank 21 in 32), the correlation of this concentration and the public concern for medical disputes in these regions are even more significant. This gives a rough support for our hypothesis about uneven distribution medical resources and migration of patients. Also, these regions are big cities and developed regions, which have relative higher medical cost.

    For the CGSS attitude questions, although not accurate, but for simplicity means the response ratings for each questions are computed to show the average opinions in each provinces.  Using red to indicate negative views on doctors, while green for positive, we present the results  in similar map forms as the Baidu Index in Figure 5. In this figure, we can observe that the average views reported from these 4 questions show consistent patterns across regions. Moreover, the level of negative views on doctors also tend to be higher in regions with more searches for medical disputes. This result thus enhances the validity to use the responses of these 4 questions as indicators of trust on doctors and perception of doctor-patient relationship. In particular, although with similar variation scale, the responses for the 2 questions on the top are relatively more negative than the 2 on the bottom. This shows a greater concern for accidents and finance.related medical disputes.

4.2 Regression results

4.2.1 Baidu Index on Provincial Level

Figure 7. Fitted Results from SLR of Baidu Index

Figure 8. Regression Table for Baidu Index

   

 Figure 7 and Figure 8 jointly present the results of a simple linear regression of Baidu Index searches on doctors’ burden, medical cost, and concentration of Grade A hospitals. All the three factors show clear positive effects on the number of searches  of medical disputes, consistent with our analysis from descriptive evidence. Specially, the positive sign of local density of Grade A level hospitals illustrate our migration story in a more sophisticated way. The concentration of Grade A level hospitals, instead of generating discontent of medical services and intense doctor-patient relationship in the patients’ origins as assumed, also gathers the medical disputes and public concern for this in their located regions. In Figure 8 we further present the regression result with robust standard error adjusted for heteroskedasticity, and the results remain significant. This result, though simple and clear, has its prediction limitation due to small sample size and too simple model.

4.2.2 CGSS on Individual Level

Figure 9. CGSS Regression Result OLS 1

Figure 10. CGSS Regression Result OLS 2

Figure 11. CGSS Regression Result Ordered Logit 1

Figure 12. CGSS Regression Result Ordered Logit 2

    For the CGSS attitude questions on views about doctors, we first conduct OLS regressions on individual level. For the key independent variables, the corresponding doctors’ burden, medical costs and Grade A hospitals in the province the respondent live are matched to each individual respondent as the medical service conditions they encounter in their medical experience. Some most relevant individual controls are added including income level, education level and self-reported medical insurance protection level. Figure 9 and 10 show some of the results in these regressions. The significant level is not perfect after adding controls, but the effect of doctors’ burden, medical cost and concentration of Grade A hospitals shows to demine general trust to doctors and increase intense medical relationships.

    To better model the scaling responses of values in the survey questions, we further use the ordered logit model to see the effects of our explanatory variables. Figure 11 and Figure 12 present the results of ordered logit regression. The standard error in the parentheses are adjusted to be robust for the clustering of provincial level data. The results here further support our previous results, yet with higher statistical significance and better explanatory power. The result of inpatient and outpatient medical cost are both significant at 5% level, while those of the doctors’ burdens and Grade A level hospitals are less significant as in the OLS model. To interpret the results more vividly, consider the estimation results in column (3) and (6) in Figure 11. With all the other factors unchanged, an increase of total outpatient cost per visit by 20 RMB will increase the possibility to report lower trust level on doctors by about 5%, while an increase of the inpatient cost by 2000 RMB will increase the probability by about 7%. This may not seem to be very large based on the deviation in our limited data about average medical cost of 32 provinces, but in reality the medical cost may vary much larger even within the same province. Due to this, this effects of medical cost deserve our notice.

    Another interesting result shown in all the above regressions is that the medical insurance has a very significant effect to reduce medical disputes and encourage trust on doctors. Here we need to notice that the medical insurance protection level used in the regression is a self-report rating of the question “How well are you protected by your medical insurance?”. A higher self perceived protection level of medical insurance may relax patients’ worry about the finance and security during a treatment, the two most concerned factors.This may explain the positive effect of this term. Besides, this protection level shows no evidence of correlation with different types of medical insurance one enjoys, namely public or commercial when tested in the sample. This implies a more detailed research is needed for what characteristic of medical insurance influence its protection level on the beneficiaries.

4.2.3 Satisfaction of Treatment and Trust on Doctors

Figure 13. Regressions on Satisfaction OLS 1

Figure 14. Regressions on Satisfaction OLS 2

Figure 15. Regression Result before and after Adding Satisfaction in Ordered Logit Model

    Our last assumption is about the the treatment expectation factor. We tried to look for statistics on “number of family members involved in healthcare occupations” as proxies for the reasonability of patients’ treatment expectation. But depressingly, the data was not complete. To move a step backward, although we could not direct measure how rational a patient is in general, we are able to measure how well their expectations are fulfilled during the past experience. By using “the satisfaction level of the last visit to doctor” as the key explanatory variable in this section, we are trying to test how an unpleasant treatment experience, even just one time, will influence people’s trust for the whole doctor group here. The OLS results are shown in Figure 13 and 14. A high satisfaction level of last visit to doctors contribute significantly to a higher reported trust level on doctors. In the other way around, unsatisfactory medical experience below one’s expectation will raise the medical disputes possibility. Figure 15 reports the ordered logit regression results before and after adding satisfaction into regressions on medical costs. After including satisfaction, the coefficient and significant level of medical cost both decrease, which shows a hint that the satisfaction of past medical experience may be an important intermediate channel to transfer the effect of medical cost on people’s views about doctors.

5. Conclusion

    In conclusion, our paper combine literature evidence, descriptive data and regression estimation to prove the several causes proposed. According to our result, higher medical cost, higher hospital concentration, and higher doctor burden all impose positive effect on the social medical dispute level and mistrust of doctors with their significance level rank in a descending order. In addition, another subjective factor - impression and satisfaction about the last visit to doctors - also proves a positive effect on trust level. This indicates that, in addition to the three rather objective factors, psychological level factors also could not be neglected when considering the dispute causes. Interestingly, we also come up with an extra finding in extension to our initial hypothesis: a better medical insurance protection could significantly offset the negative effects of other factors through offering more security senses.

    Based on these findings, we suggest several feasible policies for government to better perform medical dispute control. First, the medical cost in both public and private hospitals should be revisited and carefully priced. Mischarging, over-prescription and corruption in hospitals with weak administration capital should be red flagged and some essential yet expensive treatments should be proportionally subsidized. What’s more, the government should establish better communication channels between patients and healthcare workers in order to minimize the information asymmetry and misunderstanding. Setting central online complaint platforms and allocating sufficient human capital to giving timely feedback on these platforms would be a good choice. Last but not least, a much more sophisticatedly designed and affluently funded social medical insurance structure is highly desired.
